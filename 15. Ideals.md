# Setting

Let $R$ be a ring with unity $1_R$.

An **ideal** of $R$ is a subset $I$ of $R$ such that
- $I$ is a subgroup of $(R, +)$
- $I$ is closed under multiplication by elements of $R$, i.e. for all $r \in R$ and $a \in I$, $ra \in I$ and $ar \in I$.

--- 

# Operations on ideals 

Let $I$ and $J$ be ideals of a ring $R$.

- $I + J := \{i + j \mid i \in I, j \in J\}$
- $IJ := \{ij \mid i \in I, j \in J\}$

Let $\{I_j\}_{j \in S}$ be a family of ideals of $R$.
- $\bigcap_{j \in S} I_j := \{x \in R \mid x \in I_j \text{ for all } j \in S\}$
- $\sum_{j \in S} I_j := \{ \sum_j i_j \mid i_j \in I_j \text{ for all } j \in S \text{ and } \text{all but finitely many } i_j \text{ are zero}\}$


---
# Maximal Ideal

$I$ is called a maximal ideal of $R$ if $I$ is a proper ideal of $R$ and there is no other ideal $J$ of $R$ such that $I \subsetneq J \subsetneq R$.

---

# Theorem
Every non-zero ring $R$ has a maximal ideal.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

**Proof** (using Zorn's Lemma):
For any chain $\mathcal{C}$ of proper ideals of $R$, let 
$$U := \bigcup_{I \in \mathcal{C}} I. $$

Then $U$ is a proper ideal of $R$ since $1_R \notin U$.

Clearly, $U$ is an upper bound for the chain $\mathcal{C}$.

By Zorn's Lemma, there exists a maximal element $M$ among all proper ideals of $R$.
</details>

# Partially ordered set

A **partially ordered set** (or **poset**) is a set $P$ together with a binary relation $\leq$ that satisfies:

1. **Reflexivity**: $a \leq a$ for all $a \in P$
2. **Antisymmetry**: If $a \leq b$ and $b \leq a$, then $a = b$
3. **Transitivity**: If $a \leq b$ and $b \leq c$, then $a \leq c$

**Examples:**
- $(\mathbb{N}, \leq)$ with the usual ordering
- $(P(X), \subseteq)$ where $P(X)$ is the power set of a set $X$
- The set of ideals of a ring ordered by inclusion

---

# Zorn's Lemma


**Zorn's Lemma**: Let $P$ be a partially ordered set. If every chain (totally ordered subset) in $P$ has an upper bound in $P$, then $P$ contains at least one maximal element.

**Key concepts:**
- A **chain** is a subset $C \subseteq P$ where any two elements are comparable
- An **upper bound** of a chain $C$ is an element $u \in P$ such that $c \leq u$ for all $c \in C$
- A **maximal element** $m \in P$ is one where there is no $x \in P$ with $m < x$

**Note**: Zorn's Lemma is equivalent to the Axiom of Choice and is a fundamental tool in algebra for proving existence of maximal objects (like maximal ideals, bases of vector spaces, etc.).


---

# Theorem 
$R$ is a commutative ring with unity.
If $M$ is a maximal ideal of a ring $R$, then $R/M$ is a field (i.e. every non-zero element is a unit).

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

Every non-zero element of $R/M$ has the form 
$$\bar{a} := a + M$$ 
where $a \notin M$. 

$M$ is maximal $\Rightarrow$ $(a) + M = R$
$\Rightarrow$ there exists $r \in R$ such that $ar + m = 1_R$. 
$$\Rightarrow \bar{a} \bar{r} = (a + M)(r + M) = ar + M = 1_R + M = \bar{1}.$$

Similarly, one can show left inverse exists.
</details>

---

# Alternative Proof

Let $\varphi: R \to R/M$ be the canonical projection.

$\bar{a}$ is non-invertible $\Rightarrow$ $(\bar{a}) \subsetneq R$
$\Leftrightarrow$ $\varphi^{-1}((\bar{a}))$ is a proper ideal of $R$ containing $M$.

This contradicts the maximality of $M$.

# Prime Ideal


A **prime ideal** of a ring $R$ (not necessarily commutative) is a proper ideal $P$ of $R$ such that:

For all ideals $I, J$ of $R$, if $IJ \subseteq P$, then $I \subseteq P$ or $J \subseteq P$.

---

# Lemma 

When $R$ is commutative, this is equivalent to:
- For all $a, b \in R$, if $ab \in P$, then $a \in P$ or $b \in P$.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>



($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $P$ is a prime ideal and $ab \in P$ for some $a, b \in R$. 

$$(a)(b) = (ab) \subseteq P \Rightarrow (a) \subseteq P \text{ or } (b) \subseteq P.$$
Therefore $a \in P$ or $b \in P$.

Remark: We need $R$ to be commutative for $(a)(b) = (ab)$.


</details>

---


($\Leftarrow$) Let $I, J$ be ideals of $R$ with $IJ \subseteq P$.

Assume $I\not \subseteq P$ and $J \not\subseteq P$. Then there is $i\in I-P$ and $j\in J-P$ such that $ij\in IJ \subseteq P$.
The condition implies $i \in P$ or $j \in P$.
This is a contradiction. 

---

# Completely Prime Ideal

A **completely prime ideal** of a ring $R$ (not necessarily commutative) is a proper ideal $P$ of $R$ such that:
For all $a, b \in R$, if $ab \in P$, then $a \in P$ or $b \in P$.

## Theorem

If $P$ is a completely prime ideal of a ring $R$, $P$ is a prime ideal.  

---

# Theorem 

If $P$ is a completely prime ideal $\Leftrightarrow$ $R/P$ has no zero divisors. 

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>
For all $a,b \in R$,

- $ab \in P \Leftrightarrow \bar{a}\bar{b} = \overline{ab} = \overline{0}$. 

- $a\in P$ or $b\in P \Leftrightarrow \bar{a} = \overline{0}$ or $\bar{b} = \overline{0}$. 


</details>

---

$P$ is a prime ideal $\Leftrightarrow$ if the following holds:
- $\forall a,b\in R$ such that $arb \in P$ for all $r\in R$ $\Rightarrow a\in P$ or $b\in P$. 

---

# Theorem 

If $M$ is a maximal ideal of a commutative ring $R$, then $M$ is a prime ideal.

<details>
<summary>Proof</summary>

$M$ is maximal $\Rightarrow$ $R/M$ is a division ring
$\Rightarrow$ $R/M$ has no zero divisors
$\Rightarrow$ $M$ is prime. 

</details>

# Chinese Remainder Theorem 

Let $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_k$ be pairwise coprime positive integers (i.e., $\gcd(n_i, n_j) = 1$ for $i \neq j$).

For any integers $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_k$, the system of congruences
$$\begin{cases}
x \equiv a_1 \pmod{n_1} \\
x \equiv a_2 \pmod{n_2} \\
\vdots \\
x \equiv a_k \pmod{n_k}
\end{cases}$$

has a unique solution modulo $N = n_1 n_2 \cdots n_k$.

In other words, the ring isomorphism
$$\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/n_1\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n_2\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/n_k\mathbb{Z}$$
is given by $x + N\mathbb{Z} \mapsto (x + n_1\mathbb{Z}, x + n_2\mathbb{Z}, \ldots, x + n_k\mathbb{Z})$.

---

# Chinese Remainder Theorem (General Version)

Let $R$ be a ring with unity. 
Let $I_1, I_2, \ldots, I_k$ be ideals of $R$.

Suppose $I_i + I_j = R$ for all $i \neq j$ (i.e., the ideals are pairwise comaximal).
Then there is a ring isomorphism
$$R/(I_1 \cap I_2 \cap \cdots \cap I_k) \cong R/I_1 \times R/I_2 \times \cdots \times R/I_k$$
given by 
$$r + (I_1 \cap I_2 \cap \cdots \cap I_k) \mapsto (r + I_1, r + I_2, \ldots, r + I_k).$$
